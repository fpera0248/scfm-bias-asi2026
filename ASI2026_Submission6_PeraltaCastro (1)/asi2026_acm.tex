\begin{abstract}
Single-cell foundation models (scFMs) are increasingly used in biomedical research, yet biases can enter at every stage of the analysis pipeline, from which donors are sampled, to how cohorts are composed, to how models amplify existing imbalances. Training datasets are often strongly imbalanced across demographic variables such as sex, age, and ethnicity, and these imbalances propagate into model representations, creating demographic representation gaps where minority groups are overlooked in downstream analysis. Such biases risk producing diagnostic errors that disproportionately affect underrepresented populations, particularly as foundation models begin to influence clinical decision-making.

We address three questions in this work: Does demographic imbalance affect model learning? Which foundation model is most robust to that imbalance? And which rebalancing strategy most reliably restores equity?

We evaluate three foundation models (scFoundation, Geneformer, scGPT) on three cohorts spanning a wide range of demographic imbalance: an interstitial lung disease cohort, a colorectal cancer atlas, and the Asian Immune Diversity Atlas. We compare four strategies for addressing data imbalance: proportional sampling, downsampling, upsampling, and realistic augmentation based on the scDesign3 model, and benchmark them against Harmony with the demographic group as the batch variable. We find that all models exhibit poor integration on proportional data, with representation gaps scaling with demographic imbalance, and provide practical guidelines for selecting rebalancing strategies in real-world settings.\end{abstract}

% --- INTRODUCTION ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

Single-cell foundation models (scFMs) aim to transform biomedical research by learning universal representations of cellular states from millions of transcriptomic profiles~\cite{hao2024scfoundation,theodoris2023geneformer,cui2024scgpt}. These models achieve state-of-the-art performance on gene expression enhancement, cell clustering, and drug response prediction, and have emerged as widely used tools for zero-shot tasks across tissues and disease contexts. Since scFMs are deployed directly as frozen encoders without retraining, any patterns encoded during pretraining propagate unchanged into downstream representations, making the quality of their training data critical.

The datasets on which these models are trained reflect structural inequities that accumulate along the entire analysis pipeline~\cite{willem2025bias}. Societal biases determine which populations have access to clinical care and thus which donors contribute samples. Cohort biases arise when studies oversample certain demographic groups, as is common in genomics~\cite{popejoy2016genomics}. When ML models are trained on such imbalanced datasets, they prioritize the overrepresented group while overlooking diversity within others~\cite{willem2025bias}, and in healthcare this has led to algorithms that systematically underestimate the care needs of minority patients~\cite{obermeyer2019dissecting}. Fairness in single-cell genomics remains largely unexplored despite these well-documented risks. ML models trained on imbalanced data often fail to generalize to underrepresented groups, a phenomenon termed the ``generalization gap''~\cite{das2022efficient,arpit2017memorization}. In embedding space, minority classes occupy compressed regions and mix poorly with majority classes~\cite{dablain2022efficient}. We extend this concept to single-cell genomics, defining a \textit{demographic representation gap} as the quantifiable difference in a model's internal representation quality across demographic groups.

Several approaches to addressing demographic imbalance have been explored in single-cell and machine learning contexts. Adaptive Resampling (AR)~\cite{navidi2025adaptive} re-weights training examples so that cells in underrepresented regions of the embedding receive more influence during learning, improving minority-class accuracy in VAE-based models. Expansive Over-Sampling (EOS)~\cite{dablain2022efficient} generates synthetic minority examples by extrapolating away from majority-class neighbors in embedding space. Both operate at inference time and can be layered on top of any embedding, making them natural complements to data-level interventions. Their effectiveness depends on what the encoder has retained, bounding gains on frozen scFMs that already collapse minority structure.

Algorithmic fairness research has proposed multiple definitions of model fairness. One criterion asks whether all groups should receive positive predictions at the same rate, while another asks whether the model should be equally accurate across groups. These criteria often conflict, meaning that satisfying one can require violating another~\cite{hardt2016equality,chouldechova2017fair,kleinberg2016inherent}. None of these approaches have been evaluated head-to-head across multiple frozen foundation models, where the representation bottleneck may limit their effectiveness and where model choice may matter as much as strategy choice.

Leading scFMs consistently note the absence of bias or subgroup analysis as a key limitation~\cite{hao2024scfoundation,zeng2025cellfm,baek2025single}, and no systematic benchmark exists that compares foundation models and rebalancing strategies across embedding quality, downstream classification, and generalization diagnostics jointly. Closing this gap requires joint evaluation across multiple metric families and multiple model architectures, since no single metric or single model captures equity behavior on its own. Data-centric resampling has proven effective in single-cell settings, yet such methods require model retraining, which is computationally prohibitive for multi-million parameter foundation models~\cite{navidi2025adaptive}.

In this work, we present a structured audit framework demonstrated on three foundation models (scFoundation~\cite{hao2024scfoundation}, Geneformer~\cite{theodoris2023geneformer}, scGPT~\cite{cui2024scgpt}) to evaluate demographic imbalance across ethnicity, age, and sex axes on three cohorts spanning a range of imbalance regimes: an interstitial lung disease (ILD) cohort with severe ethnicity imbalance (European American to Native American, 127:1), a colorectal cancer (CRC) atlas with moderate skew (European American to African American, 17:1), and the Asian Immune Diversity Atlas (AIDA), a near-balanced healthy reference cohort (Korean to Indian, 8.4:1). We ran the full audit on all three demographic axes and three cohorts. We focus on ethnicity in the main text because it spans the widest range of imbalance, and report age and sex patterns where they confirm or extend the ethnicity findings.

We incorporate scDesign3~\cite{song2023scdesign3} to generate covariate-conditioned synthetic cells that expand underrepresented combinations of cell type and demographic group. Unlike oversampling or latent-space interpolation, scDesign3 operates directly in gene-expression space and preserves gene-to-gene correlation structure while conditioning on demographic features such as ethnicity or age. We compare this augmentation strategy against proportional sampling, naive upsampling, and downsampling. The framework asks three questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item[(Q1)] Does demographic imbalance affect model learning across architectures?
    \item[(Q2)] Which scFM is most robust to that imbalance?
    \item[(Q3)] Which rebalancing strategy most reliably restores equity across models and cohorts?
\end{itemize}

All three models inherit demographic segregation from input data, with baseline integration performance tracking imbalance ratio regardless of architecture. Geneformer is the most equity-robust of the three on 5 of 6 cohort-axis combinations tested, while scFoundation is the weakest on every combination. Among rebalancing strategies, augmentation with scDesign3 improves minority cell-type recovery on multi-group ethnicity axes under severe imbalance and preserves real-cell distributions, with the magnitude of improvement depending on the imbalance regime and model architecture. Downsampling produces nominally higher integration scores through small-N artifacts (per-group cell counts so low that local neighborhoods mix by construction), and upsampling matches augmented worst-group accuracy through duplicate-cell memorization that fails the within-cell-type mixing diagnostic.

By evaluating how demographic imbalance shapes scFM behavior across architectures and cohorts, and by characterizing when each rebalancing strategy helps and when it does not, this work provides an actionable framework for fairness auditing in single-cell analysis. Foundational representations that reflect diverse populations are essential for building equitable, reliable AI systems that support biomedical research and clinical decision-making across all patient groups.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Datasets and Sampling Strategies}

\textbf{Interstitial lung disease cohort.} We analyze scRNA-seq data from 
Natri et al.~\cite{natri2024ild}, comprising 471{,}905 cells across 31{,}432 
genes from 105 unique donors. We retain cells with complete demographic 
metadata for each axis of interest, yielding an analysis pool of 398{,}735 
cells across 96 donors after QC. Age is extracted from the \texttt{development\_stage} ontology 
field and binned into 10-year intervals. Disease metadata is binarized to 
normal vs.\ disease for downstream classification. The dataset has severe imbalances across 
all three demographic axes (Table~\ref{tab:demo_all}).

\textbf{Colorectal cancer atlas.} We additionally evaluate on a colorectal 
cancer (CRC) atlas~\cite{moorman2024crc} comprising 47{,}107 colorectal cancer 
epithelial cells across 25{,}344 genes (34{,}356 cells across 26 donors after QC). Sex is approximately balanced (close 
to 1:1) and excluded from analysis because there is no informative imbalance 
to evaluate. CRC serves as a complementary test of whether equity gains generalize when the imbalance ratio is milder (Table~\ref{tab:demo_all}).

\textbf{Asian Immune Diversity Atlas.} We additionally evaluate on the Asian 
Immune Diversity Atlas (AIDA)~\cite{kock2025aida}, comprising 886{,}586 
peripheral immune cells across 35{,}477 genes from 617 healthy donors. AIDA extends the evaluation to a near-balanced 
cohort, providing a complementary imbalance regime to ILD and CRC 
(Table~\ref{tab:demo_all}).

\textbf{Sampling strategies.} For each cohort-axis combination, we construct 
four dataset variants, each isolating a specific mechanism of demographic 
rebalancing. Since the full cohorts are too large to process through scDesign3 
fitting and three foundation model embeddings in a single pass, we work with 
pilot subsets that preserve the original group proportions, approximately 
2{,}500 cells per pilot across ILD, CRC, and AIDA. The four variants are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proportional baseline:} Cells are sampled proportionally 
    by demographic group, preserving the original class imbalance. This 
    baseline quantifies the extent of bias in foundation model embeddings on 
    typical imbalanced datasets and serves as the anchor against which all 
    other strategies are compared.

    \item \textbf{Balanced augmented:} scDesign3~\cite{song2023scdesign3} 
    generates synthetic cells for minority groups until each group reaches 
    parity with the majority group, conditioned on cell type and demographic 
    group. The model fits negative binomial marginals per gene and a Gaussian 
    copula on the top 1{,}000 highly variable genes, then simulates new cells 
    whose joint expression distribution matches the per-group, per-cell-type 
    real data. This strategy tests whether adding genuine new biological 
    variation can close the representation gap without model retraining.

    \item \textbf{Balanced upsampled:} Demographic parity is achieved through 
    random duplication with replacement. This variant introduces no new 
    biological diversity and serves as a lower bound that tests whether numeric 
    balance alone is sufficient. Since the frozen encoder produces identical 
    embeddings for identical input, duplicated cells stack at exactly the same 
    coordinates in latent space, making upsampling a particularly informative 
    control for memorization effects.

    \item \textbf{Downsampled:} All groups are reduced to the minority group 
    size via random undersampling. This represents the theoretical ceiling of 
    what perfect balance alone can achieve, at the cost of discarding the vast 
    majority of available data. For ILD ethnicity, this means 16--48 cells per 
    group. For CRC age, it is 99 cells per group. At these scales, classifier 
    training is severely data-limited.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Validation sets.} For each cohort-axis combination we sample one 
external validation set, held out before any pilot construction and shared 
across all four rebalancing strategies. ILD validation sets are stratified 
equally across demographic groups so that per-group performance estimates have 
comparable statistical power. AIDA validation sets are similarly stratified. 
CRC validation sets use all available held-out real cells because the CRC pool 
is too small to stratify at comparable per-group depth. Full validation set 
sizes are shown in Table~\ref{tab:val_sets}.

Sharing the validation set across strategies corrects an earlier per-strategy 
split that leaked duplicated cells from upsampled training partitions into

appears to outperform every other strategy on internal accuracy. Under external 
validation, the same upsampled pilots collapse to majority-class prediction on 
held-out donors.

\subsection{Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}

We process each model and cohort-axis combination through one shared pipeline. 
We generate synthetic cells using scDesign3 on the top 1{,}000 highly variable 
genes, producing the four dataset variants described above. Library size for synthetic cells is computed from the full 
gene matrix rather than the HVG subset to avoid systematic underestimation of 
total counts.

Each dataset variant is embedded through three frozen foundation models. For 
\textbf{scFoundation}, we sort raw integer count vectors descending by 
expression value, truncate or zero-pad to 15{,}000 genes, and pass them 
through the frozen backbone in batches of 64 cells with float16 
mixed-precision inference. The final representation is the mean-pooled 
768-dimensional embedding per cell. These settings follow scFoundation's recommended embedding extraction~\cite{hao2024scfoundation}. For \textbf{Geneformer V2-316M}, we 
tokenize cells via the Genecorpus-104M dictionary using 
\texttt{TranscriptomeTokenizer} (\texttt{version="V2"}), then embed with \texttt{EmbExtractor} producing 1{,}152-dimensional mean-pooled cell 
embeddings from the second-to-last layer, excluding CLS and EOS tokens. This is the default Geneformer V2 cell embedding pipeline~\cite{theodoris2023geneformer}. For 
\textbf{scGPT}, we tokenize gene-value pairs with a CUDA-visible runtime and 
extract the 512-dimensional CLS-token embedding per cell. This follows scGPT's recommended cell-level embedding setting~\cite{cui2024scgpt}. All three models are 
deployed as frozen encoders with no fine-tuning.

We use the scIB benchmark~\cite{luecken2022benchmarking} for integration metrics. iLISI is the primary metric, rescaled to $[0,1]$: 1.0 indicates perfect demographic mixing in each cell's neighborhood, 0.0 complete segregation. NMI and ARI score unsupervised clustering of the embedding against ground-truth cell-type labels. cLISI measures local cell-type purity in the same neighborhood framework as iLISI, where values near 1.0 indicate clusters remain cell-type-pure. Disease labels from real cells are propagated to synthetic cells via a $k$-nearest neighbor classifier ($k = 5$, Euclidean distance) in embedding space. Real-only datasets pass through unchanged.

For downstream evaluation, we predict cell-type identity using a logistic regression classifier on each model's frozen embeddings, evaluated on the shared external validation set rather than per-strategy internal splits. We also train random forest classifiers on ILD ethnicity at different training-set sizes to track how each strategy learns over time, and report the key numbers in Section~\ref{sec:q3}. For ILD and CRC, we run a normal-vs.-disease classification as a secondary check and summarize the results alongside the main findings in Section~\ref{sec:q2}. AIDA donors are all healthy at sample collection, so cell-type classification is the only downstream task for that cohort.

\textbf{Learning curves.} Since rebalancing strategies that duplicate cells (upsampling) or generate synthetic ones (augmentation) introduce different training dynamics, we use learning curves to separate strategies that produce steady generalization from those that exit majority-class prediction only after crossing a representation threshold. Curves report macro-F1 vs.\ training-set size with 5-fold cross-validation using a random forest classifier on the frozen embeddings. Both training and validation curves are reported so that the train-validation gap is visible alongside the generalization trajectory.

\textbf{Harmony baseline.} For each cohort-axis-model combination we apply harmonypy 2.0~\cite{korsunsky2019fast} to the proportional embedding using the demographic label as the batch covariate, then compute the same scIB metrics on the Harmony-corrected embedding. Harmony rearranges existing cells via per-cluster linear offsets, in contrast with augmentation which adds novel cells.

% --- RESULTS ---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Results}

We organize the Results around the three questions defined in the Introduction, using ethnicity as the primary demonstration axis (127:1 imbalance in ILD). We verified that the same patterns hold on age and sex in all three cohorts.

\subsection{Q1: Does data imbalance affect model learning?}
\label{sec:q1}

Baseline iLISI on the proportional pilots tracks the demographic imbalance ratio of the source cohort across every model tested (Table~\ref{tab:integration}). On the severely imbalanced ILD ethnicity axis (127:1), proportional iLISI is 0.054 for scFoundation, 0.031 for Geneformer, and 0.062 for scGPT, all far below the 0.80 expected for random mixing across five groups. On CRC ethnicity (17:1), proportional iLISI rises to 0.179, 0.079, and 0.102 across the three models. On AIDA ethnicity (8.4:1), it rises further to 0.382, 0.187, and 0.248. The same ordering holds for age, with smaller absolute gaps because age imbalance is milder on every cohort. Near-zero principal-component regression (PCR) scores on the demographic label confirm that no model amplifies the demographic signal beyond what PCA already captures from the input. The segregation comes from the underlying data, and the encoder transmits it. This is a data composition problem common to all three architectural families, not a property of any single foundation model.

Figure~\ref{fig:combined_umaps} shows ILD ethnicity UMAPs of Geneformer embeddings under the four rebalancing strategies. In the proportional embedding, European American cells dominate and minority groups are sparsely scattered. Augmentation balances group representation: under BalancedAugmented, all five groups form visible clusters of comparable size because scDesign3 generates novel gene-expression profiles that Geneformer embeds at new coordinates rather than stacking copies at existing ones. Upsampled and proportional UMAPs are nearly identical because duplicated cells receive identical embeddings and occupy the same points in latent space. Downsampled at 48 cells per group is sparse but visibly more mixed.

\textbf{Augmentation lifts iLISI, with magnitude depending on encoder architecture.} On Geneformer (Figure~\ref{fig:combined_umaps}), BalancedAugmented lifts ILD ethnicity iLISI from 0.031 to 0.055, CRC from 0.079 to 0.139, and AIDA from 0.187 to 0.264. The mechanism is visible in the figure: minority groups are weakly defined in the proportional embedding and form distinct clusters of comparable size under BalancedAugmented. scFoundation shows much larger absolute lifts (ILD 0.054 to 0.260, CRC 0.179 to 0.382) because its expression-aware encoder embeds synthetic cells at new coordinates in previously empty latent regions. On AIDA, where baseline mixing is already substantially higher (0.382), scFoundation augmentation produces no further lift (0.390). Geneformer's rank-value encoding and scGPT's CLS-token aggregation map synthetic cells near existing cells, producing smaller iLISI gains even when the visible effect on minority cluster formation is comparable.

\textbf{Harmony works in the opposite regime to augmentation.} A natural question is whether existing batch correction already closes the representation gap. Applying Harmony with the demographic label as the batch variable produces an outcome that depends on the imbalance regime (Table~\ref{tab:integration}). On AIDA (mild imbalance), Harmony achieves competitive or superior iLISI to BalancedAugmented for Geneformer (0.402 vs.\ 0.264) and scGPT (0.419 vs.\ 0.303). On ILD and CRC, the ordering reverses: BalancedAugmented reaches 0.260 on ILD scFoundation vs.\ Harmony 0.077, and 0.382 on CRC scFoundation vs.\ Harmony 0.091, which falls below the proportional baseline. Harmony rearranges existing cells via per-cluster linear offsets and works when minority groups are already populated at cohort level. When minorities are sparse and structurally segregated by disease subtype, the latent regions they occupy cannot be shifted closer to majority regions through linear offsets. Augmentation handles this by adding cells in previously unpopulated regions.

NMI rises for several model-strategy combinations and cLISI remains above 0.99, confirming that local cell-type structure is preserved. ARI drops under augmentation on multiple combinations, which we attribute to a Leiden over-fragmentation artifact rather than a loss of biological signal. When synthetic cells increase local density at cell-type boundaries, Leiden splits clusters more finely at a fixed resolution, dropping ARI even as each sub-cluster remains cell-type-pure.

\textbf{Downsampling produces the highest iLISI on 8 of 9 ethnicity combinations, but for reasons that do not reflect genuine integration.} Downsampling reaches 0.640 on ILD ethnicity scFoundation, 0.766 on CRC ethnicity scFoundation, and 0.468 on ILD ethnicity scGPT. The exception is AIDA ethnicity scFoundation, where the augmented pilot (0.390) edges out the downsampled pilot (0.376). Two compounding artifacts drive the downsampling scores. First, downsampled neighborhoods are small (48 cells per group on ILD ethnicity, 92 cells per group on AIDA ethnicity), which trivializes local mixing. With five or seven groups at this scale, every $k=15$ neighborhood mixes by construction regardless of what the encoder learned, so iLISI cannot distinguish genuine integration from forced mixing. Second, random undersampling oversamples high-expression cells. On the ILD ethnicity scFoundation pilot, mean library size in the downsampled pool is 7{,}363 versus 6{,}255 in proportional (KS$=$0.189, $p < 0.001$). High-expression cells embed more diffusely in latent space, which inflates iLISI further. The AIDA scFoundation exception fits the same mechanism in reverse: when the baseline embedding already mixes groups well (proportional iLISI 0.382), the downsampling artifact has less headroom to add. Downsampling also does not scale: only 240 cells remain in the pilot versus 398{,}735 available in the ILD ethnicity pool. Augmentation produces lower iLISI than downsampling on severe-imbalance cohorts but does so on full-scale pilots that preserve real-cell distributions and support downstream classification.

\subsection{Q2: Which scFM is most robust to demographic imbalance?}
\label{sec:q2}

We rank the three foundation models by worst-group cell-type macro-F1 on the proportional pilot, the strategy that preserves natural demographic imbalance and therefore reveals each model's intrinsic robustness. Worst-group F1 is the lowest per-group F1 across demographic groups in a given cohort-axis combination.

\textbf{Geneformer holds the highest worst-group F1 on 5 of 6 cohort-axis combinations.} On ILD ethnicity, Geneformer reaches 0.732 (Hispanic or Latin), scGPT 0.695 (Native American), scFoundation 0.049 (Native American). On CRC ethnicity, Geneformer 0.862 (Hispanic or Latin), scGPT 0.850 (Asian), scFoundation 0.206 (African American). On AIDA ethnicity, Geneformer 0.301 (Indian), scGPT 0.169 (Indian), scFoundation 0.087 (Indian). The same ordering holds on age in 2 of 3 cohorts: Geneformer wins ILD age and AIDA age, while scGPT beats Geneformer on CRC age (0.828 vs.\ 0.734). scFoundation is the weakest model on every cohort-axis combination tested, with worst-group F1 0.08 to 0.67 below the next model.

The cross-model gap is large enough on scFoundation that no rebalancing strategy closes it. ILD scFoundation Native American under BalancedAugmented reaches 0.073, still 0.68 below Geneformer proportional on the same group (0.751). Switching from scFoundation to Geneformer improves worst-group F1 more than any data-level intervention does. The same ranking shows up when we ask the models to predict disease status on ILD and CRC: Geneformer has the highest worst-group disease accuracy on every combination tested, confirming the cell-type ranking is not specific to one task.

The mechanism is architectural. Geneformer encodes each cell as a rank-ordered gene sequence, and relative gene order is more stable across donors than absolute counts. scFoundation's read-depth-aware pretraining normalizes sequencing depth but not the demographic-covariate imbalance baked into the source corpus, so the model preserves depth structure while still encoding donor-level expression differences that correlate with demographic background. scGPT's CLS-token aggregation produces compact embeddings that track Geneformer's mean-pool closely on proportional data, with the absolute gap between the two models small (0.01 to 0.13 worst-group F1), and scGPT exceeding Geneformer on CRC age.

\begin{answerbox}{Q2}
Geneformer is the most equity-robust of the three models tested, with the highest worst-group cell-type macro-F1 on 5 of 6 cohort-axis combinations. scFoundation is the weakest on every combination. The cross-model gap is large enough that model choice tends to dominate strategy choice for equity outcomes.
\end{answerbox}

\subsection{Validating Synthetic Cells from scDesign3}
\label{sec:scdesign3_results}

We address two concerns with scDesign3 augmentation: faithfulness to real biology and inheritance of majority-group structure. Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests on the top 100 highly variable genes per (group, cell type) pair show synthetic cells reproduce real-cell expression distributions with high fidelity: the fraction of HVGs at $p < 0.05$ stays at or below 8\% across all combinations (mean 0.6\%, median KS statistic 0.000 to 0.030, Bonferroni-corrected fraction at most 4\%). Logistic regression classifiers trained on foundation-model embeddings achieve AUC$=$1.000 at every scope (overall, per cell type, per demographic group), meaning synthetic cells carry a separable technical signature despite matching real cells at the gene level. To test whether this signature is majority-derived, we train an additional classifier to distinguish synthetic minority cells from pooled real-other-group cells (excluding the matched minority): AUC$=$1.000 across all 9 ethnicity combinations in both directions. Synthetic cells are equally distinguishable from real cells in every group, not preferentially similar to majority structure. The technical signature most likely reflects scDesign3's parametric sampling (capture inefficiency, ambient RNA, and count tails that negative binomial and Gaussian copula do not perfectly reproduce) and does not invalidate the iLISI gains under augmentation.

% --- DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION --------------------------------------------
\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

The central finding of this work is that demographic bias in scFM embeddings is a data composition problem whose severity scales with source data imbalance. Across all three cohorts and all three models tested, baseline iLISI tracks the imbalance ratio of the underlying axis. Strategy choice depends on the model, the cohort, and the imbalance regime. No single rebalancing strategy dominates across all settings.

\textbf{When augmentation matters and when it does not.} Synthetic augmentation provides clear iLISI gains on disease cohorts with severe demographic imbalance (ILD 127:1, CRC 17:1), where minority cells occupy latent regions Harmony cannot reach through linear offsets. On the milder-imbalance AIDA cohort (8.4:1), Harmony achieves competitive or superior iLISI to BalancedAugmented for Geneformer and scGPT, and augmentation produces minimal further gain. Harmony rearranges existing cells via per-cluster offsets and works well when minority cells are already present in latent space at sufficient density. Augmentation adds cells in regions where the source data lacks coverage. Practitioners should select the intervention to match the imbalance regime rather than treating either as universally appropriate. On bootstrap-corrected per-group cell-type macro-F1, augmentation produces a statistically separable gain on ILD Native American with scFoundation (0.049 [0.04, 0.06] to 0.073 [0.06, 0.10], non-overlapping CIs). On AIDA Indian with scGPT, point estimates rise substantially (0.169 [0.15, 0.20] to 0.244 [0.20, 0.29]) but bootstrap CIs overlap by small margins. Point-estimate comparisons without CIs consistently favor augmentation, but the per-group gain crosses statistical significance only on the most severely imbalanced minority.

\textbf{Model choice matters more than strategy choice on most combinations.} Geneformer holds the highest worst-group cell-type macro-F1 on 5 of 6 cohort-axis combinations tested. Switching from scFoundation to Geneformer improves worst-group cell-type macro-F1 by 0.56 to 0.71 across the six cohort-axis combinations, a larger gain than any rebalancing strategy delivers within a single model. The same ranking holds when predicting disease status on ILD and CRC, so the result is not specific to cell-type classification. The architectural reason, rank-value encoding instead of absolute counts, is general and likely to apply to other rank-based scFMs as they emerge.

\textbf{Biology, bias, and pretraining inheritance.} Distinguishing biological variation from learned bias is fundamentally difficult here. Performance gaps across demographic groups may reflect real biological differences in cell-type composition, transcriptional state, or disease subtype distribution at the donor level rather than pure model bias, especially on disease cohorts where minority ethnicities correlate with specific disease subtypes (ILD idiopathic vs.\ non-IPF, CRC tumor subtypes). We frame our results as evidence of model behavior under demographic shift, not as a clean measurement of model bias in isolation. The fact that segregation severity tracks input imbalance regardless of architecture suggests encoders transmit the demographic skew of their pretraining corpus directly into downstream representations. This raises concerns for any application where embedding choice affects equitable outcomes, and points to pretraining corpus composition as a fairness intervention upstream of any cohort-level rebalancing.

\textbf{Practical guidance.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Select Geneformer as the default scFM for cohorts with non-trivial demographic structure.
\item Use scDesign3 augmentation when imbalance is severe and minorities are structurally segregated (ILD).
\item Use Harmony with the demographic as batch variable when imbalance is mild and minorities are already present in latent space (AIDA).
\item On per-group F1, upsampling can match or exceed augmentation (notably CRC African American across all three models) but produces locally segregated embeddings where duplicated minority cells stack at identical coordinates, undermining downstream interpretation that depends on neighborhood mixing.
\item Avoid downsampling, which forces high iLISI through small-N artifacts and collapses overall cell-type classification.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Limitations.} Pilot datasets are small relative to full cohort scale: proportional pilots approximately 2{,}500 cells, BalancedAugmented and BalancedUpsampled reaching roughly 10{,}700 cells, and downsampled at 240 cells. Effect sizes may change at scale, and minority synthetic-to-real ratios become extreme on full cohorts (126:1 for Native American at 100K), raising tractability concerns for some augmentation regimes. Validation sets are sampled cell-wise rather than donor-wise, and some minority groups (Native American, n$=$2 ILD donors) cannot support donor-level splits at this scale. The disease-ethnicity confound on ILD and CRC is partially addressed: minority ethnicities correlate with specific disease subtypes at the donor level, and per-disease stratified analysis is needed to fully disentangle bias, biology, and disease-subtype distribution. The scDesign3 synthetic cells introduce a detectable technical signature in embedding space. While we show this signature is not majority-derived (Section~\ref{sec:scdesign3_results}), metric interpretation should account for its presence.

The framework is modular and model-agnostic, supporting systematic bias auditing for any frozen scFM as the single-cell AI landscape evolves.
